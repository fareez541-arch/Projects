## Lesson Block 1: Numbers (20 min) — 9:10

### Today's Focus: Numbers 10-15 + Counting with Purpose

**Energon Cube Counting (10 min):**
- Get 15 small objects (Lego bricks work perfectly — call them "Energon cubes")
- "The Autobots need Energon to survive! Let's count how much we have."
- Count together 1-15, touching each one.
- "Can you count them by yourself?" Let him try. Help only if he skips or double-counts.

**The Number 10 — "Optimus's Power Level" (5 min):**
- Make a group of 10. "This is 10. Optimus Prime's base power level."
- Add 1 more. "Now how many? 10... and 1 more... ELEVEN! Optimus just got stronger!"
- Continue to 15. Each number = more power.
- Write the numerals 10-15 on paper. Have him trace/copy.

**One More, One Less (5 min):**
- Put out 12 cubes. "If a Decepticon STEALS one, how many are left?" Remove one. Count.
- "If we FIND one more, how many now?" Add one. Count.
- Do this 4-5 times with different starting numbers.

**Process praise:** "You counted every single one without skipping — that takes patience and focus."

## Hands-On Activity: Energon Sort and Graph (20 min) — 9:30

### Simple Bar Graph — "How Much Energon Does Each Autobot Have?"

**Setup:**
- Get 3 different colors of small objects (red Legos, blue Legos, yellow Legos — or colored cereal, buttons, crayons)
- Draw 3 columns on paper. Label them with Autobot names (draw simple robot faces or let him draw them):
  - Red column = Optimus
  - Blue column = Bumblebee  
  - Yellow column = Ratchet

**Activity:**
1. Mix all the objects in a pile. "Oh no! The Energon got mixed up! Can you sort it by color?"
2. Let him sort into 3 groups.
3. "Now let's see who has the most. Stack the red cubes in Optimus's column."
4. Stack each color in its column (physically on the paper, or draw circles).
5. "Who has the MOST Energon? Who has the LEAST? How many MORE does Optimus have than Bumblebee?"

**This teaches:** sorting, counting, comparison, "more than/less than," and the foundation of data visualization.

**Extension:** "What if we gave Bumblebee 2 more? Would he have more or less than Optimus now?"

## Lesson Block 2: Science — Observation Mission (20 min) — 10:00

### "Autobot Recon: Nature Scout"

**Concept:** Scientists and engineers START by observing. Before you build, you watch. Before you fix, you look.

**Setup:** Go outside (yard, porch, sidewalk) OR use a houseplant/flower.

**Mission (15 min):**
1. "Today's mission: you are a SCOUT for the Autobots. Scouts observe and report. Your mission is to find something from nature and study it like a scientist."
2. Find a leaf, flower, rock, bug, or stick.
3. Bring it to the "observation station" (a table or mat).

**Five Senses Observation:**
- "What does it LOOK like? What color? What shape? Big or small?"
- "What does it FEEL like? Smooth or rough? Hard or soft? Heavy or light?"
- "What does it SMELL like?" (if applicable)
- "If you're very quiet... does it make any SOUND?" (leaves rustling, bugs buzzing)
- "We don't taste it — scouts don't taste unknown things!"

**Drawing (5 min):**
- "Now draw what you observed. Try to make it look EXACTLY like what you see."
- Observational drawing builds visual attention, fine motor, and scientific documentation skills.

**Parent guide:**
> Don't tell him what to notice. ASK questions: "What do you notice about the edges?" "Are both sides the same?" Use the word OBSERVE instead of "look" — build the vocabulary of science.

## Parent Notes for Shazeema

**What Aariz is actually learning today:**
- Number sense: counting beyond 10, understanding that each number is "one more" (cardinality)
- Data literacy: sorting, comparing quantities, reading a simple bar graph
- Scientific observation: using senses systematically, drawing from observation
- Spatial vocabulary: more/less, most/least, taller/shorter (comparing graph columns)

**Watch for:**
- Can he count to 15 without skipping? If he gets stuck at 12 or 13, that's normal — just practice
- Does he understand "one more" and "one less"? If not, use physical objects — don't go abstract yet
- During nature observation, does he notice details? If he says "it's a leaf" and stops, prompt: "what ELSE do you notice?"

**Read-aloud tonight:** Choose a book with numbers or counting if you have one. "How Many Legs?" or "Ten Black Dots" are great. Otherwise, any book — ask him to find the letter O on 3 pages.
